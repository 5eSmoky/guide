Things to Do

The headliners of the Smokies — Dollywood, The Island, moonshine, shows, mountain coasters, and the national park itself.

Dollywood

★ The signature day
theme park · ~10–12 min

One of America's most-awarded theme parks: world-class coasters, craft shows, Smoky Mountain food, and seasonal festivals. New for 2026 is NightFlight Expedition, an immersive indoor adventure coaster. Plan a full day, and check the calendar for operating days and festival dates.

Tip: arrive at opening, hit headline rides first, save shows for midday heat Plan & tickets: dollywood.com

The Island in Pigeon Forge

Free to stroll
~7 min · day & night

A 23-acre entertainment hub with the 200-ft Great Smoky Mountain Wheel, a dancing fountain show, shops, snacks, an arcade, and Ole Smoky Moonshine. Easy, walkable, and free to wander.

Best for: an easy evening with the whole group islandinpigeonforge.com

Moonshine & Distillery Tastings

Adults
flights & tours

Tennessee's legal-moonshine country. Sample at Ole Smoky (at The Island), Old Forge Distillery, Tennessee Shine Co., and Junction 35. Most offer cheap tasting flights and short tours.

Tip: tastings fill up on weekend evenings — go earlier

Dinner Shows

Dinner + entertainment
book ahead

Dolly Parton's Stampede (horses & a four-course meal), the Hatfield & McCoy Dinner Feud (comedy + Southern feast), and Pirates Voyage (acrobatics on a full-size ship). Loud, fun, and a hit with all ages.

Best for: a no-cooking-tonight family evening

Great Smoky Mountains National Park

Free entry · ~15 min
scenic drives, waterfalls & hikes

The most-visited national park in the country is right at your doorstep. Drive Cades Cove (an 11-mile loop famous for wildlife), Newfound Gap, and the Roaring Fork Motor Nature Trail; hike to Laurel Falls, Clingmans Dome (Kuwohi), or Andrews Bald. There's no entrance fee, but a parking tag is required to stop (see Good to Know).

Tip: go early for wildlife and parking; Cades Cove jams up by mid-morning nps.gov/grsm

Mountain Coasters & Ziplines

Thrill
all over the Parkway

Gravity-fed alpine coasters you control yourself, plus zipline canopy tours. Try the Smoky Mountain Alpine Coaster, Rowdy Bear, Goats on a Roof, and Smoky Mountain Ziplines.

Live Shows & Comedy

Evenings
family-friendly

Beyond the dinner shows, the Parkway is lined with theaters — magic, music, and the long-running Comedy Barn. A relaxed, indoor option for a cooler or rainy night.

Best for: an easy night out of the weather

Day Trip: Gatlinburg

About 10 minutes south, where the Parkway meets the national park. An easy half- or full-day of mountaintop views and downtown strolling.

Gatlinburg SkyLift Park & SkyBridge

★ The view
~10 min · downtown Gatlinburg

Ride a chairlift up Crockett Mountain to the SkyDeck, then walk the SkyBridge — at 680 feet, the longest pedestrian suspension bridge in North America, with glass-floor panels in the middle. 2026 admission runs about $30 adults / $22 kids (ages 4–11), including the chairlift.

Tip: sunset is stunning; buy combo tickets online if pairing with another attraction gatlinburgskypark.com

Anakeesta

Mountaintop park
half to full day

A gondola or chairlift climbs to a mountaintop park with a 16-bridge Treetop Skywalk, the Anavista Tower, ziplines, a mountain coaster, gardens, and dining. Genuinely great for all ages.

Best for: views, kids, and a little thrill anakeesta.com

Ripley's Aquarium of the Smokies

All ages
88 River Road

10,000+ sea creatures, a glass underwater tunnel with sharks and rays, and hands-on touch tanks. One of the area's top rainy-day picks for families.

Best for: little ones & a weather backup

Ober Mountain (Ober Gatlinburg)

Year-round

Ride the tram up Mount Harrison for skiing and snow tubing in winter, or the alpine slide, mountain coaster and wildlife encounter the rest of the year.

Great Smoky Arts & Crafts Community

The largest group of independent artisans in the country — 100+ studios and galleries along a scenic loop just east of downtown. Pottery, leather, brooms, candles, and more.

Downtown Parkway & The Village

Stroll & sip

Moonshine and whiskey tastings (Sugarlands, Ole Smoky), the European-style Village Shops, candy makers, and live music at spots like Ole Red. Easy to wander on foot.

Season-by-Season

What to expect in the Smokies all year, plus the standout events each season.

Spring

March to May
  • Wildflowers everywhere — the park is nicknamed "Wildflower National Park"
  • Dollywood reopens for the season; smaller crowds than summer
  • Mild days, cool nights — pack layers
  • Late May/June: the famous synchronous fireflies at Elkmont (lottery access)
Fresh, green, and the calm before the summer rush.

Summer

June to August
  • Peak season: Dollywood's Splash Country open, everything running
  • Warm and humid with brief afternoon thunderstorms
  • Book Dollywood, dinner shows and tours ahead — they sell out
  • July 4th fireworks and festivals along the Parkway
Busy, lively, and the classic full-tilt family vacation.

Fall

September to November
  • Leaf season is the big one — color usually peaks mid-to-late October
  • Dollywood Harvest Festival & Great Pumpkin LumiNights
  • Crisp, gorgeous hiking weather
  • Heads-up: October weekends are very busy — book early and start days early
Arguably the most beautiful time to be in the mountains.

Winter

December to February
  • Millions of lights: Dollywood's Smoky Mountain Christmas & Winterfest area-wide
  • Ober Mountain skiing & snow tubing; Pigeon Forge Snow year-round
  • Best deals and the shortest lines of the year
  • Snow/ice is possible — check mountain road conditions before higher drives
Cozy, festive, and a great-value getaway.

Bear country basics, mountain-road sense, the national-park parking tag, and the house rules.

🐻 Bears & Wildlife

You're in black-bear country — the park is home to roughly 1,500 black bears, and they do wander into neighborhoods and cabins. A few rules keep you and the bears safe.

  • Never feed or approach wildlife. In the park it's illegal to willfully come within 50 yards (150 ft) of a bear or elk.
  • Secure all food and trash. Don't leave food, coolers, or leftovers on the deck, by the grill, or visible in your car.
  • Take trash to the bins promptly and keep lids closed; don't toss food scraps outside.
  • If you see a bear, keep your distance and enjoy it from afar — use your phone's zoom, not your feet.
  • If a bear approaches: stay calm, pick up small children, make yourself look big, and back away slowly. Do not run.
  • Black bears are not grizzlies — if one actually attacks, do not play dead. Fight back, aiming for the face and muzzle.

🚗 Mountain Roads & the National Park

You're a flat minute from the Parkway, but day trips climb into the mountains. A little planning goes a long way.

  • Mountain roads are winding and can fog up — slow down, take breaks, and go easy on cruise control.
  • The national park has no entrance fee, but a "Park It Forward" parking tag is required to stop for more than 15 minutes: about $5/day, $15/week, or $40/year.
  • Buy tags online at recreation.gov or smokiesinformation.org, at visitor centers, or at REI on Teaster Lane. Print it — digital tags aren't accepted. Just driving through is free.
  • The Parkway strip backs up midday in season; mornings are calmer, and the Gatlinburg bypass skips some traffic.
  • Mountain weather changes fast and runs cooler at elevation — bring a layer even in summer.
